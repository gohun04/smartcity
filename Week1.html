Working in the office


In this topic we will be looking at the office and the important things you need to know about working in the gaming industry. As part of a team you will be making games with budgets in the millions of dollars. For this to happen there are ways things are done that ensures the process is as smooth as possible.

Key players

Making large video games is a team effort and it's important to know the key people who you will be working with, especially as you may be spending long hours trying to hit a deadline. In a gaming studio you will often be making assets that will be used by other team members. Some key people are...

3D Artist

The 3D artist is responsible for creating 3d assets for the game. A 3D artist has a general knowledge in creating 3D models and may have other roles such as texturing in a small studio. They will need skills in topology, polygon efficiency and general design principles (that you will learn about later).  3D artists can become a specialist in modelling certain things that require different types of skills. For instance a 3-D artist who creates models for characters will need to know anatomy and how the body needs to be shaped for animation, where a modeller for creating buildings will need to know about hard surface modelling. Some common areas of expertise for 3-D artists are characters, vehicles, buildings, weapons and environments.

Character Artist

The character artist creates the characters used in games. They can use a variety of techniques including digital sculpting and box modelling. Character artists need the same skills as a 3D artist plus knowledge in anatomy and movement. Character artists should also have a good understanding of design principles such as silhouette, form and function.

Texture Artist

The texture artist provides the material and colours that are placed on top of the models. They use a variety of tools such as Photoshop to create 2D textures, and substance painter to paint directly onto the model. Texture artist may work with a large range of textures from plants and trees to skin and fur.

Software used:

Maya, 3DS Max, Mudbox, Z Brush, Cinemea 4D, Blender, Photoshop, Substance Painter

Animator

Animators take the 3D models and make them move. There may be 2-D animators in the gaming filed, but most will be 3-D. Animators can become specialist in fields such as character animation, special effects animation, vehicles and more. They should have knowledge of anatomy and movement as well as the principles of animation to add realism to the movements of objects within the game.

Software used:

Programmer

Programmers add the interaction to the game by creating the code or codebase.  It is the programmer's job to implement assets, mechanics and physics into the game. Programmers may work generally on the game more specifically on areas such as the game engine, AI, sound, physics, graphics, gameplay and more.

Game designer

The game designer designs how the game will work. They come up with the rules that run the gameplay and have skills and understanding in many areas of the game development process. It's up to the game designer to define the core elements of the game. The game design document that they create will be used by all members of the development team.

Testers/ QA

The people who test and document the quality assurance of a game are known as the testers. It is their job to find faults in the game on a technical and design level. The role of game tester is often misinterpreted as a role where you do nothing but play games all day. While the tester will be playing the game, they must be playing with a critical mind looking for bugs and trying to break the game. The tester may have to play the same level many, many times.

Pipeline

The production pipeline is the process assets go through from concept to completion. The pipeline will be different for each studio, but no matter where you are the process should be simular. Watch the video to learn about a typical 3d production pipeline.

           Stages of a typical game production pipeline:

The Concept: The basic idea of the game is conceived. This idea could come from anywhere and be based on anything. If the idea is considered good then the next phase begins.

Pre- Production :

The pre- production phase sees the writers, designers, artists and programmers get together to create the game design document, story, storyboards and concept art. At this stage a prototype may be made of the game.

Game storyboards copyright blizzard entertainment

Production :

With the game design document completed, or nearly completed the game moves into the production phase. Here we see more artist, designers and programmers brought in. The artists will now be creating models and animations. The programmers will be creating game systems and the designers will be constantly looking at the game design document and making any changes as soon as possible.

Production modelling copyright blizzard entertainment

Post- Production :

The game is then put together and enters the final stage of development. The game will go through multiple creation and testing phases including the alpha and beta versions. When all the testing is done and all the bugs have been ironed out the game is ready for release. Depending on the platform of the game, covers will be made and the game will be manufactured and distributed to be sold.

Each part of the pipeline will have its own separate pipeline such as the character modelling pipeline.

Scheduling and Customer expectations

Keeping to a schedule is important in the digital media industry. Projects that don't stick to the schedule can be expensive, as more money will need to be pumped into a project that runs over time. Video games will often have a release date put in place many months before the game is finished. If the game isn't finished by the allocated date you will be letting down your customers from the people waiting to play your game to the producers and others involved in the games development.

A common scheduling practice within the video game development industry is to use a Gantt Chart. Gantt charts represent the project schedule in a bar chart. The bar shows the start and end date of each production piece.

Communication

Good communication skills are important. While working on video games you will need to share information and ideas with your other team members. To make sure the project is completed on time and follows the design good communication is needed and you will be doing that through several communication methods.

Verbal Communication

Verbal communication uses speech to communicate. It requires the use of tone and stress cues to portray what the speaker is communicating.

Non-verbal Communication

Non-verbal communication uses techniques such as body language, facial features and even the cloths you wear.

Written Communication

Written communication uses words to communicate your message. This could be in a written letter, email or blog post.

Listening Skills

Listening is an essential skill for communication. By practicing good listening skills you will better understand what is being communicated to you. You can improve your listening skills by looking at the person speaking to you, don't speak while they are speaking and by repeating and asking questions about what is being said.



Copyright

Copyright protects the intellectual property (IP) of your game. By gaining copyright you can protect your creations and stop other people from claiming them for themselves. As a worker within the video game industry it is important that you don't use material such as models from other games, in your work as this would be a violation of copyright law.

More information about copyright including the copyright act can be found at http://www.ag.gov.au/RightsAndProtections/IntellectualProperty/Pages/Copyright-information.aspx

 

You can apply for a copyright at http://www.ipaustralia.gov.au/get-the-right-ip/copyright/

KPI

KPI or key performance indicators are a way to see what goals need to be met for the organisation to be successful. The KPI's in game development may refer to the amount of units that need to be sold or how many monthly users are needed to keep a game profitable.
